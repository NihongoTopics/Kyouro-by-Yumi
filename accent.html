Kyouro: Accent

Explained by Yumi • Derived strictly from the PDFs in this repo

Scope and Sources

This page summarizes how Kyouro treats accent, using only the materials in this repository: Proposing a New Romanization System of Japanese and Semantics of Tokyo Japanese. No outside knowledge is used.

Prosodic Units & Tones

The PDFs define a strict prosodic hierarchy: mora → syllable → accent phrase (AP) → intonation phrase (IP) → sentence. A sentence contains whole IPs; an IP contains whole APs; an AP contains whole syllables; a syllable contains whole moras.

A mora bears one of three contour tones: rising (R), level (Lv), and falling (F). R and Lv together are called nF (non‑falling). Allocation of nF vs F is lexical. The position of accents in an AP determines which moras receive nF and which receive F. R vs Lv is determined by syntax/information structure; every sentence has at least one R.

An AP begins with one or more nF moras and ends with zero or more F moras. If an nF follows an F, an AP boundary lies between them. Boundary falls can appear between APs.

How to Read This

Kyouro connects spelling with phonological behavior and structure. Within that design, accent is handled through the items above. The manuscripts position accent alongside related concepts such as AP boundaries and boundary falls (also listed in the table of contents), which suggests interaction points; this page keeps to what is explicitly stated in the PDFs.

Accent Symbols

  • Upper accent (modifier letter vertical line): phonetically active; responsible for surface tones.
  • Lower accent (modifier letter low vertical line): phonetically inactive “goal” position; does not itself affect surface tones.
  • Boundary fall (modifier letter grave accent): steep pitch fall between adjacent nF moras.
  • Lower boundary fall (modifier letter low grave accent): trace of an unrealized boundary fall or an AP boundary without a phonetic fall inside a tokenized form.

Mechanics inside an AP

  • For each accent within an AP, the mora immediately after the accent and all moras on the left receive nF; the rest receive F. (See “Proposing a New Romanization System of Japanese,” p. 42.)
  • This makes the rightmost accent in the AP phonetically effective; earlier accents are redundant for surface tones.
  • Intuitively: moras default to F; accents assign nF to the leftward stretch from the accent.

Accent Numbers

An AP (or relevant form) has an accent number N referring to position from the right edge of its segmental sequence:

  • N = 0: accent at the right segmental edge (immediately after the ultimate mora).
  • N = 1: immediately behind the rightmost mora; N = 2: behind the penultimate mora; and so on.

Constraints noted in the PDFs:

  • Verb stems: accent numbers are only 0, 1, or 2 (1 is marginal). Revealed when followed by items like ta.
  • Adjective stems: 0, 1, or 2. N=2 is almost exclusively for two‑mora stems; N=1 mostly in three‑mora or longer stems. Revealed when followed by items like ku.

Suffixes and Multiple Accents

Some suffixes carry an accent or interact with nF assignment, so a single AP can contain multiple accents (e.g., a stem plus a suffix). The PDFs give these generalizations:

  • -kara extends the nF sequence when the preceding item’s accent number is 0. (p. 42)
  • -no extends the nF sequence when the preceding item’s accent number is 1 and the preceding item’s accent is not on the left edge. (p. 42, “The lower accent”)
  • The rightmost accent within the AP is phonetically effective; earlier accents mark structure but do not override that outcome.

Citations refer to “Proposing a New Romanization System of Japanese,” page 42.

The Lower Accent: Moving an Accent

Certain items can move the immediately preceding form’s accent to a specified position P (the lower accent). The PDFs summarize the condition schematically:

Move A (the preceding form’s accent) to P iff A is not initial in that form and is either final in that form or within one mora of P.

Examples in the PDFs:

  • -kara extends the nF sequence when the preceding item’s accent number is 0. (p. 42)
  • -no extends the nF sequence when the preceding item’s accent number is 1 and the accent is not at the left edge. (p. 42)
  • -katta can move an adjective stem’s accent leftward when the stem’s accent number is 0.

Notation in tokenized form: P is marked by the lower‑accent symbol (modifier letter low vertical line). If movement happens, P is then marked by the upper‑accent symbol to show phonetic effectiveness, and the original position is marked as a trace.

Accent Shift

Kyouro includes a section on “accent shift,” indicating that accent position can change under defined conditions. The manuscript discusses when and how such shifts occur, within Kyouro’s own framework.
