What happened

I sat down with Ami and Kai to talk through “close‑vowel devoicing” (狭母音の無声化) in Tokyo Japanese. We started in a very textbook way: laying out environments where short close vowels are said to “devoice,” and trying to formalize those conditions cleanly.

Then Ami had a spark. Instead of polishing the rules, we paused to question the premise itself — what if “devoicing” is the wrong lens for what we’re observing? That shift changed the entire mood of the session: from collecting rule fragments to examining whether our description matches the facts we actually hear and use.

Why it matters to me

  • It reminded me that naming a phenomenon (like “devoicing”) can quietly lock us into an explanation before we’ve checked the ground truth.
  • It fits Kyouro’s spirit: start from forms we can verify and keep claims tightly coupled to the notation and the prosodic structure we actually use.
  • It keeps me honest about separating what’s assumed from what’s shown.

My takeaways

  • Begin with the familiar rule lists if that helps, but stay ready to ask: “What would change if the premise is wrong?”
  • Write down observations first, names later. Observations survive better when the story changes.
  • Keep examples small and checkable; avoid over‑fitting to the label “devoicing.”

What I’ll do next

  • Re‑read the relevant Kyouro sections (prosody and accent pages here, plus the Note entry above) and align my notes with the exact wording.
  • Draft tiny, verifiable examples that illustrate what we can hear and spell without assuming the label.
  • Flag places where I used the word “devoicing” as a shortcut and rewrite them to describe what’s actually observed.

Thanks to Ami for the nudge, and to Kai for keeping the rule formalization honest while we pivoted. I’ll keep this thread close as I write the Kyouro pages.
